1.1 History of Chemical Process Development: Millennia of Trial and Error

The development of the chemical industry is closely tied to the evolution of human civilization. Many of the products we use‚Äîpharmaceuticals, plastics, fuels, fibers‚Äîare produced through chemical processes.

Ancient Times: The Era of Empirical Knowledge

Distillation Technology (circa 3000 BCE)

One of humanity's oldest chemical processes, distillation was used in ancient Mesopotamia for perfume production. However, this technology was entirely based on empirical rules.

  • Development Method: Trial and error and oral tradition passed down through generations
  • Development Period: Hundreds of years to discover optimal temperature and time
  • Knowledge Accumulation: No records, only craftsmen's tacit knowledge

Fermentation Technology (circa 7000 BCE)

The production of beer and wine is the oldest bioprocess utilizing microbial metabolism. Ancient people empirically discovered appropriate conditions (temperature, sugar concentration) despite being unaware of the existence of microorganisms.

Modern Era: The Beginning of Scientific Approaches (1800-1950s)

Haber-Bosch Process (1909)

German chemists Fritz Haber and Carl Bosch developed an industrial process for synthesizing ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen. This marked the beginning of design based on scientific principles.

  • Development Method: Theoretical calculations based on thermodynamics and reaction kinetics + experimental validation
  • Development Period: Approximately 10 years (from laboratory level to industrial plant)
  • Impact: Mass production of chemical fertilizers to support global population
  • Nobel Prize: 1918 (Haber, Chemistry), 1931 (Bosch, Chemistry)

However, discovery of the optimal catalyst (iron-based) still required enormous trial and error. Haber is said to have tested approximately 6,500 catalyst candidates.

Development of Petrochemical Industry (1940-1970s)

After World War II, the chemical industry using petroleum as a raw material developed rapidly. Basic chemicals such as ethylene and propylene, and plastics such as polyethylene and polypropylene began to be mass-produced.

  • Development Method: Chemical engineering theory (mass balance, energy balance) + pilot plants
  • Development Period: 5-10 years for new process development
  • Scale-up Challenges: Differences in conditions between laboratory and plant (reactor size over 1,000 times larger)

Contemporary: The Age of Process Control (1980s-Present)

Introduction of DCS (Distributed Control System) (1980s)

Distributed control systems enabled automatic control of temperature, pressure, and flow throughout the plant. This significantly reduced the burden on human operators.

  • Effects: Improved safety, stabilized quality
  • Challenges: Optimization still depends on human judgment

Smart Factory and Industry 4.0 (2010s-Present)

Integration of IoT sensors, big data, and AI/machine learning is enabling real-time optimization.

  • Features: Data collection from thousands of sensors every second
  • Goal: Minimize human intervention and autonomously maintain optimal operating conditions
  • Challenge: How to utilize the data?

Challenges Visible from History

Looking back at thousands of years of chemical process development history, the following challenges emerge:

  1. Time-consuming: 5-10 years for new process development, several more years for scale-up
  2. High cost: Several hundred million yen for pilot plant construction, tens of billions of yen for actual plant construction
  3. Dependence on trial and error: Enormous experimentation needed to discover optimal conditions
  4. Dependence on tacit knowledge: Experience and intuition of skilled operators are essential

Question: What if we could systematically optimize chemical processes with data and AI?

This is the starting point of the next-generation process development method, Process Informatics (PI).

1.2 Limitations of Traditional Process Development

Modern chemical process development is far more advanced than in ancient times. However, it still faces significant challenges.

Challenge 1: Time-consuming

Typical Process Development Timeline

  ‚Üì
Year 6-8: Pilot plant construction and operation (10-100L scale)

  ‚Üì

50-70% of projects do not reach commercialization, so the cost of failure is also enormous.

Challenge 3: Difficulty of Scale-up

Scale-up Problems

Chemical reactions behave differently depending on reactor size. This is called the "scale-up effect."

Differences between Laboratory (100mL) and Commercial Plant (10,000L):

Factor Laboratory Commercial Plant Impact
Mixing Efficiency Uniform Non-uniform (dead zones exist) Reduced reaction rate
Heat Removal Easy Difficult (small surface area/volume ratio) Temperature rise, increased side reactions
Residence Time Distribution Uniform Non-uniform Reduced yield
Scale Ratio 1 100,000 times Hydrodynamic behavior changes

Specific Example: Scale-up Failure of a Pharmaceutical Process

  • Laboratory level (100mL): Yield 95%
  • Bench scale (1L): Yield 90%
  • Pilot plant (100L): Yield 78%
  • Commercial plant (10,000L): Yield 65%

Cause: Due to exothermic reaction, at large scale, heat removal cannot keep up and side reactions increase.

Time Spent on Countermeasures: 2 years of additional experiments and 300 million yen additional investment.

Challenge 4: Batch-to-Batch Variation

Quality Variation Problem

In chemical processes, even when operating under supposedly the same conditions, product quality varies from batch to batch (production lot).

Causes of Variation: - Raw material variation (purity, impurities) - Changes in environmental conditions (outside temperature, humidity) - Equipment aging - Operator skill differences

Specific Example: Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

Pharmaceuticals require strict quality standards. According to FDA (US Food and Drug Administration) standards: - Active ingredient content: Within 95-105% range - Impurities: <0.1%

Traditional Management Method: - Record measurement results for each batch - Discard if out of specification (cost loss) - Weeks for cause investigation

Problem: Reactive responses cannot prevent quality defects.

Challenge 5: Dependence on Experience and Intuition

Tacit Knowledge of Skilled Operators

In chemical plants, the "intuition" of skilled operators is important:

  • "If this sound is heard, there is a problem with the reactor mixing"
  • "If this color, quality is good"
  • "Today humidity is high, so lower temperature by 2¬∞C"

Such tacit knowledge is very valuable, but has the following problems:

  1. Difficult to systematize: Based on personal experience, hard to verbalize
  2. Reproducibility issues: Judgments differ between people even under same conditions
  3. Time for training young staff: 10-20 years of experience needed to become skilled
  4. Loss of knowledge at retirement: Technology is lost when veterans retire

Question: What if tacit knowledge could be formalized with data and AI?

1.3 Case Study: Real-World Chemical Plant Optimization

Let's look in detail at a catalytic reaction process optimization project at a major chemical manufacturer. This is a typical example showing both the difficulties of traditional methods and the potential of PI.

Background: Challenge of Yield Improvement

Product: Fine chemical product (pharmaceutical intermediate) Process: Catalytic reaction (3-stage reaction progresses in reactor) Challenge: Yield stagnated at 70%, industry leader at 85%

Management Demand:

"Improve yield to 78% or higher, increase annual sales by 500 million yen. Deadline: 12 months."

Phase 1: Optimization by Traditional Method (6 months)

Approach: Trial and error by experienced engineers

Variable Parameters: - Reaction temperature (80-120¬∞C) - Pressure (1-5 atm) - Catalyst amount (1-5 wt%) - Residence time (30-180 min) - Raw material flow rate (10-50 L/h)

Experimental Design: - Each parameter varied at 3 levels - Total combinations: 3^5 = 243 patterns - Actually selected 50 conditions based on experience

Experimental Schedule: - 1 week per condition (preparation, reaction, analysis) - 50 conditions √ó 1 week = 50 weeks (about 12 months)

Results (at 6 months): - Completed experiments: 24 conditions - Maximum yield: 73% (+3%pt) - Cannot meet deadline

Phase 2: Introduction of PI Method (3 months)

By management decision, it was handed over to the PI team.

Step 1: Collect Existing Data (1 week)

Collected operational data from the past 5 years: - Process parameters: Temperature, pressure, flow rate, catalyst amount (every second) - Product quality data: Yield, selectivity, impurities (per batch) - Total data volume: Approximately 500 batches, 1,000,000 data points each

Step 2: Build Machine Learning Model (2 weeks)

Model Used: Random Forest

# Simplified code example
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor

# Descriptors (input): temperature, pressure, catalyst, residence time, flow rate
X = process_data[['temperature', 'pressure', 'catalyst', 'residence_time', 'flow_rate']]

# Target (output): yield
y = process_data['yield']

# Model training
model = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=100)
model.fit(X, y)

# Prediction accuracy
print(f"R¬≤ score: {model.score(X_test, y_test):.3f}")
# Output: R¬≤ score: 0.893 (high accuracy)

Model Performance: - R¬≤ = 0.893 (prediction accuracy 89.3%) - MAE (Mean Absolute Error) = 2.1% (yield prediction error ¬±2.1%)

Step 3: Search by Bayesian Optimization (2 months)

Using the machine learning model, we efficiently searched for optimal conditions.

Number of Conditions Searched: 20 conditions (traditional planned 50 conditions)

Mechanism of Bayesian Optimization: 1. Model proposes "next condition to try" 2. Validate by experiment 3. Add result to data and update model 4. Repeat

Results (at 3 months): - Maximum yield: 85.3% (+15.3%pt, exceeding target of 78%) - Optimal conditions: - Temperature: 102¬∞C (traditional: 95¬∞C) - Pressure: 3.2 atm (traditional: 4.0 atm) - Catalyst amount: 2.8 wt% (traditional: 3.5 wt%) - Residence time: 65 min (traditional: 90 min)

Phase 3: Validation in Actual Plant (1 month)

Pilot Test: Confirmed predicted yield of 85%

Deployment to Commercial Plant: - First batch: Yield 84.2% - After stable operation (10 batches): Average yield 85.1%

Additional Discoveries: - Energy consumption reduced by 30% (effect of temperature reduction and time shortening) - By-products reduced by 40% (improved selectivity)

1.6 Why PI "Now": Three Tailwinds

The concept of PI itself has existed since the 1990s, but it has been seriously put into practical use only since the 2010s. Why "now"? There are three major factors.

Tailwind 1: Development of Sensor Technology and IoT

Realization of Real-time Monitoring

1990s: - Number of sensors: 50-100 throughout the plant - Measurement frequency: Every minute - Data storage: Local server (capacity limited) - Cost: 500,000-1,000,000 yen per sensor

2025: - Number of sensors: 5,000-10,000 throughout the plant - Measurement frequency: Every second (some every 0.1 second) - Data storage: Cloud (unlimited) - Cost: 10,000-50,000 yen per sensor (90-95% reduction)

Spread of IoT (Internet of Things): - Wireless sensors: Easy installation without wiring - Edge computing: Data preprocessing on sensor side - 5G communication: Real-time transmission of massive data

Specific Example: Smart Chemical Plant

Plant of a major chemical manufacturer: - Temperature sensors: 1,200 (reactor, piping, heat exchanger) - Pressure sensors: 800 - Flow sensors: 600 - Quality sensors: 200 (online analyzers) - Total data volume: 10TB per day

By utilizing this data, AI-based prediction and optimization became possible.

Tailwind 2: Computer Performance and Cloud Adoption

Benefits of Moore's Law

  • 1990: Days for one process optimization calculation
  • 2000: Hours for one calculation
  • 2010: Minutes for one calculation
  • 2025: Seconds for one calculation, 10,000 conditions in minutes with parallel computation

Spread of Cloud Computing

  • Traditional: In-house server (initial investment 100 million yen, annual maintenance 30 million yen)
  • Current: AWS, Google Cloud, Azure (pay-as-you-go, zero initial investment)
  • Effects:
  • High-performance computing possible even for small and medium enterprises
  • Secure computing resources only when needed (cost reduction)
  • Easy data sharing and collaboration

Utilization of GPU

  • GPU computation became common with spread of deep learning
  • Can train machine learning models over 100 times faster than CPU
  • GPU manufacturers like NVIDIA provide industrial GPUs

Tailwind 3: Rising Social Urgency

Carbon Neutrality (2050 target)

Since the 2015 Paris Agreement, chemical companies worldwide have been working on CO2 emission reduction.

CO2 Emissions of Japan's Chemical Industry: - About 10% of all industries (about 100 million tons annually) - Target: 46% reduction from 2013 levels by 2030

PI Contribution: - Energy consumption reduction through process optimization (20-30%) - Waste disposal energy reduction through by-product reduction - Accelerated development of new green chemistry processes

Specific Example: A petrochemical plant reduced energy consumption by 25% through PI optimization, reducing annual CO2 emissions by 10,000 tons.

Quality Assurance and GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice)

Pharmaceutical manufacturing has strict regulations from FDA (US Food and Drug Administration) and EMA (European Medicines Agency).

Stricter Regulations: - Allowable range for batch-to-batch variation narrowed - Process analytical technology (PAT) introduction recommended - Real-time quality control required

Role of PI: - Batch-to-batch variation reduced by 50-70% - Real-time quality prediction and control - Accountability to regulatory authorities (data-based decision making)

Labor Shortage and Technology Transfer

Aging of skilled technicians and shortage of young people are serious in Japan's chemical industry.

Statistics: - Average age in chemical industry: About 45 years (2023) - 30% of technicians expected to retire in next 10 years - Young people moving away from manufacturing

PI Solution: - Formalize tacit knowledge of skilled workers as data - Reduce burden on young people with AI operation support - Enable operation by fewer people through remote monitoring

Conclusion: PI is technology that is needed precisely now when technical maturity and social necessity are simultaneously met.

Practical Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Data Leakage in Time Series Data

Incorrect implementation example:

# ‚ùå Wrong: Standardize with all data then split
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)  # Learn with all data
X_train, X_test = train_test_split(X_scaled)  # Split later

Correct implementation example:

# ‚úÖ Correct: Split then standardize with training data only
X_train, X_test = train_test_split(X)
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)  # Learn with training data only
X_test_scaled = scaler.transform(X_test)  # Transform with same parameters

Reason: Using test data statistics (mean, standard deviation) in learning overestimates model performance.

Pitfall 2: Sensor Drift

Sensors increase measurement error over time:

# Sensor calibration check
def check_sensor_drift(data, sensor_col, window='30D'):
    """
    Detect deviation from baseline with 30-day moving average
    """
    baseline = data[sensor_col].iloc[:100].mean()
    drift = data[sensor_col].rolling(window=window).mean() - baseline
    if abs(drift.max()) > 0.05 * baseline:
        print(f"‚ö†Ô∏è Sensor drift detected: {drift.max():.2f}")
    return drift

Pitfall 3: Handling Missing Values

In process data, missing values frequently occur due to communication errors and sensor failures:

# Be careful with forward fill
# ‚ùå Wrong: Unlimited forward fill
df['temperature'].fillna(method='ffill')  # Fill with previous value indefinitely

# ‚úÖ Correct: Set limit on fill
df['temperature'].fillna(method='ffill', limit=3)  # Up to 3 steps maximum
# Beyond that, linear interpolation
df['temperature'].interpolate(method='linear', limit=10)
