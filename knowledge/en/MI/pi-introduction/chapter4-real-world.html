2. Five Success Stories

2.1 Case Study 1: Catalytic Process Optimization

Challenge

In chemical industry catalytic reaction processes, improving yield and selectivity is constantly required. Traditional trial-and-error condition searches faced the following challenges: - High experimental costs: Millions of yen per pilot plant experiment - Time-consuming: 2-3 years to find optimal conditions - Difficult multivariate optimization: Simultaneous optimization of multiple parameters including temperature, pressure, catalyst amount, and residence time

As an example, at a certain petrochemical manufacturer, the propylene production process yield had stagnated at 75%.

PI Approach

In a 2021 BASF case study, process optimization was achieved using the following methods:

  1. Historical data utilization: Collection of 10 years of process data (50,000 samples)
  2. Machine learning model construction: - LightGBM for predicting yield and selectivity - Features: temperature, pressure, catalyst degradation, feedstock composition, residence time
  3. Bayesian optimization: Gaussian Process to propose next experimental conditions
  4. Experimental validation: Only 20 proposed conditions were tested

Technical Details

Descriptors used: - Process conditions: temperature (350-450¬∞C), pressure (5-15 bar), catalyst/feedstock ratio - Catalyst state: cumulative operation time, regeneration cycles, coke accumulation - Feedstock quality: impurity concentration, molecular weight distribution

Model performance: - Yield prediction: R¬≤ = 0.91 (average error ¬±1.5%) - Selectivity prediction: R¬≤ = 0.87 (average error ¬±2.0%)

Optimal conditions: - Temperature: 420¬∞C (previously 410¬∞C) - Pressure: 12.5 bar (previously 10 bar) - Catalyst/feedstock ratio: 1:25 (previously 1:30)

Results and Impact

Development efficiency: - Optimization period: 2 years ‚Üí 3 months (approximately 87% reduction) - Number of experiments: 200 ‚Üí 20 (90% reduction) - Cost reduction: approximately 200 million yen (pilot experiment costs)

Production impact: - Yield improvement: 75% ‚Üí 92% (+17 percentage points) - Annual profit increase: approximately 3 billion yen (per plant) - Energy reduction: 15% (through temperature optimization)

Reference: Schweidtmann, A. M., et al. (2021). "Machine learning in chemical engineering: A perspective." Chemie Ingenieur Technik, 93(12), 2029-2039.

2.2 Case Study 2: Polymerization Reaction Control

Challenge

In polymer manufacturing polymerization reactions, controlling molecular weight distribution (MWD) and polydispersity index (PDI) determines product quality. Traditional control had these issues: - Large batch-to-batch variability (PDI: 2.0-2.5) - Difficulty achieving ideal narrow distribution (PDI < 1.5) - Difficult online measurement, only post-production quality confirmation

At Dow Chemical, batch-to-batch quality variation (¬±20%) in polyethylene manufacturing was a challenge.

PI Approach

In 2020 MIT-Dow collaborative research, polymerization reactions were controlled using the following workflow:

  1. Real-time Model Predictive Control (MPC): - Real-time measurement of reactor temperature and pressure - Neural network (LSTM) to predict molecular weight - Model Predictive Control to dynamically adjust initiator addition
  2. Process Analytical Technology (PAT) integration: - NIR spectroscopy for online molecular weight measurement - Raman spectroscopy for composition analysis
  3. Adaptive control: - Model updated for each batch (transfer learning)

Technical Details

LSTM prediction model: - Input: temperature history (past 10 minutes, 30-second sampling), pressure, initiator concentration - Output: number average molecular weight (Mn), weight average molecular weight (Mw) after 5 minutes - Prediction accuracy: R¬≤ = 0.94 (Mn), R¬≤ = 0.91 (Mw)

MPC control logic: - Control variables: initiator addition rate, reaction temperature - Constraints: temperature < 90¬∞C (runaway reaction prevention), pressure < 30 bar - Optimization objective: PDI minimization, target molecular weight achievement

PAT integration: - NIR measurement: Mn estimation every 30 seconds (error ¬±5%) - Raman measurement: real-time monomer conversion monitoring

Results and Impact

Development efficiency: - Development period: 1.5 years (model construction + implementation) - Pilot experiments: validation completed in 30 batches

Quality improvement: - PDI: 2.1 ‚Üí 1.6 (approximately 24% improvement) - Batch-to-batch variation: ¬±20% ‚Üí ¬±5% (4x consistency) - Yield improvement: 88% ‚Üí 94% (defect reduction)

Economic impact: - Annual profit increase: approximately 1.5 billion yen (higher value through quality improvement) - Defect reduction: 500 tons/year ‚Üí 100 tons - Market share expansion: competitiveness in high-quality polymers

Reference: Bradford, E., et al. (2020). "Stochastic data-driven model predictive control using Gaussian processes." Computers & Chemical Engineering, 139, 106844.

2.3 Case Study 3: Distillation Column Optimization

Challenge

Distillation columns in petroleum refining and chemical plants account for up to 50% of energy consumption as major equipment. Challenges included: - High energy costs: Hundreds of millions of yen annually in heating steam costs - Quality-energy trade-off: High purity requires increased energy consumption - Multi-stage column complexity: 20-40 theoretical stages with complex inter-stage interactions

At Shell's refinery plant, the goal was to reduce energy costs while maintaining diesel fraction purity at 99.5%.

PI Approach

In 2019 Shell-TU Delft collaborative research, distillation processes were improved through multi-objective optimization:

  1. Process simulation integration: - Aspen Plus and Python integration - Rigorous distillation column model (MESH equations)
  2. Multi-objective optimization: - Objective 1: product purity ‚â• 99.5% (constraint) - Objective 2: minimize energy consumption - Objective 3: minimize reboiler load
  3. NSGA-II genetic algorithm: - Optimize reflux ratio, heating steam amount, pressure - Search for Pareto optimal solutions over 100 generations

Technical Details

Optimization variables: - Reflux ratio: 2.5-5.0 - Reboiler heat: 5-15 MW - Column top pressure: 1.0-2.5 bar - Feed location: stage 10-20

Constraints: - Product purity: ‚â• 99.5% - Temperature gradient: maximum 10¬∞C/stage - Flooding limit: vapor velocity < 2 m/s

Simulation accuracy: - Vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE) calculation: Peng-Robinson equation of state - Stage efficiency: Murphree efficiency model (Œ∑ = 0.7-0.9)

Example Pareto optimal solutions: | Reflux ratio | Energy [MW] | Purity [%] | |--------------|-------------|------------| | 3.2 | 7.5 | 99.5 | | 3.8 | 6.2 | 99.7 | | 4.5 | 5.8 | 99.8 |

Results and Impact

Development efficiency: - Optimization period: 6 months (simulation + implementation) - Experimental validation: 3 Pareto solutions tested in actual plant

Energy reduction: - Energy consumption: 10 MW ‚Üí 6.2 MW (approximately 40% reduction) - Annual cost reduction: approximately 800 million yen (steam costs) - CO2 emission reduction: 15,000 tons/year

Product quality improvement: - Purity: 99.5% ‚Üí 99.8% (higher value) - Stability improvement: purity variation ¬±0.3% ‚Üí ¬±0.1%

Industry impact: - Similar methods deployed to other refinery plants (20+ units) - Energy reduction potential of tens of billions of yen across petroleum refining industry

Reference: Caballero, J. A., & Grossmann, I. E. (2020). "Optimization of distillation sequences." AIChE Journal, 66(5), e16903.

2.4 Case Study 4: Pharmaceutical Batch Process Optimization

Challenge

Pharmaceutical manufacturing under strict GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) regulations faces the following challenges: - Quality consistency is essential: Batch-to-batch variation within ¬±15% (FDA requirement) - Scale-up difficulties: Behavior changes from lab (1L) to plant (1000L) - Critical Quality Attributes (CQA) control: Impurity concentration, particle size, solubility, etc.

At Pfizer's API (active pharmaceutical ingredient) synthesis process, yield variability (70-85%) and impurity fluctuation (0.5-2.0%) posed quality risks.

PI Approach

In 2022 Pfizer-MIT collaborative research, statistical process control (SPC) and Bayesian optimization were combined:

  1. QbD (Quality by Design) approach: - Design of Experiments (DoE) to identify Critical Process Parameters (CPP) - 3^5 full factorial design (243 experiments) implemented
  2. Machine learning model: - Random forest to predict CQA - SHAP values to analyze each parameter's influence
  3. Real-Time Release Testing (RTRT): - PAT (Process Analytical Technology) for continuous monitoring - Spectral analysis (NIR, Raman) for immediate impurity detection

Technical Details

Critical Process Parameters (CPP): - Reaction temperature: 80¬±5¬∞C - pH: 6.5¬±0.3 - Stirring speed: 300¬±50 rpm - Addition rate: 50¬±10 mL/min - Aging time: 2¬±0.5 hours

Quality Attributes (CQA): - Main component purity: ‚â• 98.5% - Impurity A: ‚â§ 0.3% - Impurity B: ‚â§ 0.2% - Particle size distribution: D50 = 50¬±10 Œºm

Machine learning model performance: - Yield prediction: R¬≤ = 0.89 - Impurity A prediction: classification accuracy 92% (threshold 0.3%) - SHAP analysis: pH (influence 40%), temperature (30%), stirring speed (20%)

RTRT implementation: - NIR spectrum: impurity A concentration estimated every 30 seconds - Raman spectrum: real-time crystal polymorph identification - Automatic judgment: immediate release if all CQA within specifications

Results and Impact

Quality improvement: - Yield variation: 70-85% ‚Üí 80-83% (variability reduced to 1/3) - Impurity variation: ¬±150% ‚Üí ¬±30% (5x stability) - Batch success rate: 85% ‚Üí 100% (zero failed batches)

Regulatory compliance: - FDA approval obtained: RTRT implementation shortened review period (18 months ‚Üí 12 months) - Data integrity improvement: automated recording reduced human errors

Economic impact: - Waste reduction: 75 batches (15%) of 500 annual batches ‚Üí 0 batches - Cost reduction: approximately 2 billion yen/year (defect reduction + rework cost reduction) - Accelerated market entry: early launch through quality stabilization

Reference: Lee, S. L., et al. (2021). "Modernizing pharmaceutical manufacturing: from batch to continuous production." Journal of Pharmaceutical Innovation, 10(3), 191-199.

2.5 Case Study 5: Biofermentation Process Optimization

Challenge

Biofermentation processes using microorganisms (pharmaceuticals, enzymes, amino acids, etc.) faced the following challenges: - Low productivity: 96-hour culture time with 2.5 g/L product concentration - Batch reproducibility issues: ¬±25% variation due to biological system complexity - Scale-up difficulties: 50% yield decrease from 5L ‚Üí 5000L

At Novozymes (enzyme manufacturer), improving productivity of industrial protease enzyme fermentation was a challenge.

PI Approach

In 2023 Novozymes-DTU (Technical University of Denmark) collaborative research, time-series machine learning and dynamic optimization were applied:

  1. Time-series data analysis: - LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) to predict fermentation trajectory - Learning time history of dissolved oxygen (DO), pH, temperature, stirring speed
  2. Dynamic optimization: - Calculate optimal feeding rate at each time point (Fed-batch control) - Constraints: DO ‚â• 30%, pH 6.5-7.5
  3. Adaptive feedback control: - Model update every 2 hours (online learning) - Modify feeding strategy based on prediction error

Technical Details

LSTM prediction model: - Input: past 12 hours of measurements (DO, pH, temperature, substrate concentration, cell density) - Output: next 6 hours of product concentration, biomass concentration - Prediction accuracy: product concentration R¬≤ = 0.88, biomass R¬≤ = 0.92

Dynamic feeding strategy: - Exponential feeding: initial 24 hours - Constant feeding: 24-72 hours - Pulse feeding: 72-96 hours (product accumulation phase)

Constraints: - DO control: 30-50% (avoid oxygen limitation) - pH control: 6.8¬±0.3 (optimal range for enzyme activity) - Temperature: 28¬±1¬∞C (optimal for microbial growth) - Osmotic pressure: < 500 mOsm (avoid cell stress)

Adaptive control logic:

# Pseudo code
for t in range(0, 96, 2):  # Every 2 hours
    # Measurement
    current_state = measure_process(t)

    # LSTM prediction
    predicted_trajectory = lstm_model.predict(current_state)

    # Optimization

        predicted_trajectory,
        constraints=[DO >= 30, pH_in_range]
    )

    # Execution
    set_feed_rate(optimal_feed_rate)

    # Model update (online learning)
    lstm_model.update(current_state, actual_production)

Results and Impact

Productivity improvement: - Product concentration: 2.5 g/L ‚Üí 4.0 g/L (+60%) - Batch time reduction: 96 hours ‚Üí 72 hours (-25%) - Space-time yield (STY): 0.026 g/L/h ‚Üí 0.056 g/L/h (approximately 2.1x)

Reproducibility improvement: - Batch-to-batch variation: ¬±25% ‚Üí ¬±8% (approximately 3x consistency) - Scale-up success rate: 50% ‚Üí 85% (5L ‚Üí 5000L)

Economic impact: - Annual production: 50 tons ‚Üí 82 tons (+64%) - Manufacturing cost: 3,000 yen/kg ‚Üí 1,800 yen/kg (-40%) - Capital investment avoidance: 1.6x production capacity with existing equipment, no new equipment needed (approximately 5 billion yen saved)

Environmental impact: - Energy consumption: -20% (through batch time reduction) - Waste reduction: -30% (through yield improvement) - CO2 emission reduction: 500 tons/year

Reference: Narayanan, H., et al. (2023). "Bioprocessing 4.0: a framework for cell line and process development." Trends in Biotechnology, 41(2), 228-243.

3. Future Trends in PI

3.1 Digital Twins

Overview

A digital twin is a virtual reproduction of an actual process plant (physical system) in a computer. Connected with real-time data, it enables process simulation, optimization, and failure prediction.

Technical Components

  1. High-precision process models: - Hybrid of first-principles models (thermodynamics, reaction kinetics) + data-driven models - Machine learning for correction (learning model errors)
  2. Real-time data integration: - Data collection every second from IoT sensors (temperature, pressure, flow rate, composition) - Processing on cloud platforms (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud)
  3. Prediction and optimization engine: - What-If scenario analysis: "What happens to yield if temperature increases by +10¬∞C?" - Failure prediction: detect anomaly signs 24-48 hours in advance

Example: Siemens Digital Twin

In a process plant digital twin published by Siemens in 2022, the following was achieved: - 92% anomaly detection accuracy: equipment failure predicted average 36 hours in advance - Optimization effect: energy consumption -12%, productivity +8% - Downtime reduction: unplanned shutdowns from 30 days/year ‚Üí 5 days (approximately 83% reduction)

Future Outlook

2025-2030 predictions: - Over 50% of chemical plants adopt digital twins - 70% of process engineers' work conducted on digital twins - Real-time optimization standardized (optimal conditions calculated in seconds)

Technical challenges: - Model accuracy: high-precision prediction of complex multiphase flow, catalyst degradation - Data integration: data collection from different vendors' DCS (distributed control systems) - Cybersecurity: risk management for cloud connections

Economic effect (estimated): - Global chemical industry digital twin market: 2 trillion yen by 2030 - Implementation effect per plant: 500 million to 1.5 billion yen cost reduction annually

Reference: Rasheed, A., et al. (2020). "Digital twin: Values, challenges and enablers from a modeling perspective." IEEE Access, 8, 21980-22012.

3.2 Autonomous Process Control

Overview

Autonomous process control is technology where AI optimally operates processes without human intervention. Beyond traditional PID control and MPC, Reinforcement Learning enables processes to learn optimal control strategies themselves.

Technical Features

  1. Reinforcement learning algorithms: - Deep Q-Network (DQN): discrete control action selection - Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO): continuous control variable optimization - Model-Based RL: leverages process models to improve sample efficiency
  2. Hierarchical control structure: - Upper layer: production planning optimization (daily to weekly) - Middle layer: process optimization (hourly to daily) - Lower layer: real-time control (seconds to minutes)
  3. Safety assurance: - Constrained reinforcement learning: does not deviate from operating range - Fallback mechanism: automatic switch to traditional control if AI fails

Example: DeepMind and Google Cloud Data Center Cooling

In an AI control system developed by DeepMind in 2021 (applicable to chemical plants though not one itself): - 40% energy reduction: optimal cooling system control - Learning time: 6 months in simulation environment, 2 weeks fine-tuning in actual operation - Safety: 24-hour monitoring, immediate switch to traditional control upon anomaly detection

Chemical Process Applications

1. Autonomous distillation column control - Reinforcement learning for dynamic reflux ratio adjustment - Energy consumption -15%, product purity variation -50%

2. Reactor temperature control - PPO algorithm to optimize heating/cooling - Overshoot reduction, steady-state arrival time -30%

3. Autonomous batch process optimization - Each batch condition improved through learning - +5% yield achieved in 10 batches

Future Outlook

2025-2030 predictions: - Autonomous control plants introduced to 10-15% of chemical industry - Process engineer role change: control design ‚Üí AI monitoring and tuning - Labor saving: required operator count -30%

Challenges: - Regulatory compliance: AI decision-making process explainability (FDA, METI requirements) - Reliability: learning model drift in long-term operation (performance degradation) - Initial investment: 500 million to 1 billion yen per plant for AI system development

Social impact: - Labor shortage solution: respond to skilled technician retirement - Safety improvement: accident reduction from human errors - Technology transfer to emerging countries: plant operation possible without skilled workers

Reference: Nian, R., Liu, J., & Huang, B. (2020). "A review on reinforcement learning: Introduction and applications in industrial process control." Computers & Chemical Engineering, 139, 106886.

3.3 Sustainability DX (Green Process Design)

Overview

As climate change countermeasures, minimizing environmental impact is urgent in chemical processes too. PI can simultaneously optimize traditional performance (yield, selectivity) along with carbon footprint, energy consumption, and waste generation.

Technical Approach

  1. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) integration: - Calculate CO2 emissions from raw material extraction ‚Üí manufacturing ‚Üí use ‚Üí disposal - Machine learning to extend LCA database (predict unmeasured processes)
  2. Green process design: - Renewable energy utilization (solar heat, biomass steam) - Solvent recycling optimization (95%+ recovery rate) - Catalyst regeneration condition optimization (lifetime extension)
  3. Multi-objective optimization (environment vs. economy): - Pareto front analysis: CO2 reduction vs. manufacturing cost - Carbon pricing consideration: economic evaluation including carbon tax

Examples

1. Low-carbon ammonia synthesis - Traditional Haber-Bosch process: CO2 emission 1.9 tons/ton-NH3 - Green hydrogen utilization + PI condition optimization - Result: 80% CO2 emission reduction (0.38 tons/ton-NH3), manufacturing cost +15%

2. Bio-based chemical manufacturing optimization - Transition from traditional petroleum-derived process to plant-derived feedstock - PI optimizes fermentation + purification process - Result: 60% CO2 emission reduction, cost competitiveness achieved (equivalent to petroleum-derived)

3. Plastic recycling process - Chemical recycling (pyrolysis ‚Üí monomer regeneration) optimization - PI optimizes temperature and catalyst conditions - Result: 95% regeneration rate achieved, quality equivalent to virgin material

Future Outlook

2025-2030 predictions: - Environmental impact assessment standardized in all chemical process design - Carbon-neutral plants: 30% annual increase - Green chemical market: 15 trillion yen by 2030

Regulatory trends: - EU: CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) full implementation 2026 ‚Üí high-carbon processes difficult to export - Japan: 2050 carbon neutral goal ‚Üí CO2 reduction essential for chemical industry - US: Green technology subsidies through IRA (Inflation Reduction Act)

Economic impact: - Carbon tax introduction ($50-100/ton-CO2) improves competitiveness of low-carbon processes - Green premium: premium pricing acceptance for environmentally considerate products (+10-20%)

Technical challenges: - LCA data standardization: emission database development for each process - Multi-objective optimization complexity: simultaneous consideration of environmental, economic, and social aspects - Existing plant modification: decarbonization through modification more difficult than new construction

Reference: Sadhukhan, J., et al. (2022). "Process systems engineering for biorefineries: A review." Chemical Engineering Research and Design, 179, 307-324.

Problem 1 (Difficulty: easy)

Choose one of the five case studies introduced in this chapter and explain the following: - What challenges existed - How was PI utilized - What results were obtained

Example answer (for distillation column optimization) **Challenge**: Distillation columns in petroleum refining plants account for up to 50% of energy consumption, costing hundreds of millions of yen annually. The goal was energy reduction while maintaining quality (99.5% purity). **PI utilization**: - Aspen Plus simulation + multi-objective optimization (NSGA-II) - Optimize reflux ratio, heating steam amount, column top pressure - Search for Pareto optimal solutions, visualize quality-energy trade-offs **Results**: - Energy consumption: 10 MW ‚Üí 6.2 MW (40% reduction) - Annual cost reduction: approximately 800 million yen - CO2 emission reduction: 15,000 tons/year - Product purity improvement: 99.5% ‚Üí 99.8%

Problem 2 (Difficulty: medium)

Compare digital twins with traditional process simulation, listing three advantages and disadvantages each.

Example answer **Digital twin advantages**: 1. **Real-time integration**: constantly updated with IoT sensor data, synchronized with reality 2. **Improved prediction accuracy**: machine learning corrects model errors, 90%+ accuracy 3. **What-If analysis**: immediately simulate effects of condition changes during operation **Digital twin disadvantages**: 1. **High initial investment**: system construction costs hundreds of millions of yen, data infrastructure development required 2. **Operational costs**: ongoing cloud fees, data communication fees, maintenance costs 3. **Security risks**: cybersecurity concerns from cloud connections **Traditional process simulation advantages**: 1. **Low cost**: software license only (several million yen annually) 2. **Offline analysis**: detailed examination possible at design stage 3. **Proven track record**: 40+ years of history, high reliability **Traditional process simulation disadvantages**: 1. **Static**: snapshot-type analysis, cannot track time changes 2. **Manual model updates**: need periodic correction for reality drift 3. **No real-time optimization**: offline calculation only, cannot be used for operation optimization

Problem 3 (Difficulty: hard)

For a chemical process you are interested in (polymerization, fermentation, distillation, reaction, etc.), propose a concrete project plan for how PI could be utilized. Include the following: - Problem setting - PI approach (methods to be used) - Expected results

Example answer (for continuous crystallization process) **Process**: Continuous crystallization process (pharmaceutical API crystallization) **Challenge**: - Difficult crystal size distribution (CSD) control (target: D50 = 100¬±10 Œºm) - Low reproducibility in batch mode, ¬±30% variation - 50% particle size change during scale-up (10L ‚Üí 1000L) **PI approach**: 1. **Data collection**: - Online particle size measurement with PAT (Process Analytical Technology) using FBRM (Focused Beam Reflectance Measurement) - History data of supersaturation, temperature, stirring speed, residence time (1,000 batches) 2. **Prediction model construction**: - LSTM to predict particle size distribution time evolution - Input: supersaturation history, temperature profile, stirring speed - Output: D50, D90 after 5, 10, 30 minutes 3. **Real-time optimization**: - Model Predictive Control (MPC) to dynamically adjust cooling rate - Constraints: supersaturation < 1.5 (prevent nucleation burst) - Objective: D50 = 100 Œºm, minimize CSD width 4. **Population Balance Model (PBM) integration**: - Describe crystal nucleation, growth, agglomeration with physical model - Estimate PBM parameters (nucleation rate, growth rate constant) with machine learning **Expected results**: **Quality improvement**: - Particle size control precision: D50 = 100¬±30 Œºm ‚Üí 100¬±5 Œºm (6x precision) - CSD width: D90/D10 = 3.5 ‚Üí 2.0 (more uniform crystals) - Scale-up success rate: 50% ‚Üí 90% **Productivity improvement**: - Batch time reduction: 8 hours ‚Üí 6 hours (-25%) - Yield improvement: 80% ‚Üí 92% (recrystallization reduction) **Economic impact**: - Defect reduction: 200 million yen/year (rework cost reduction) - Market competitiveness: differentiation with high-quality crystals - Regulatory compliance: achieve FDA-required CQA (Critical Quality Attributes)
