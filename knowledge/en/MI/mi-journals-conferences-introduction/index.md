## FAQ - Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: Which conference should I attend?

**A** : It depends on your career stage and research content.  
**Beginners (Undergraduates to 1st-Year Master's)** : We recommend starting with domestic conferences like the Society of Materials Science Japan or MRS-Japan. Participation fees are low (2,000-5,000 yen for students), Q&A can be conducted in Japanese, and networking is easier.  
**Intermediate (2nd-Year Master's to Doctoral)** : Challenge yourself with international conferences like MRS (Materials Research Society) or MRM (Materials Research Meeting). English presentation experience is essential during doctoral studies.  
**Advanced (Postdocs and above)** : Aim for oral presentations and invited talks at large international conferences like E-MRS and IUMRS-ICAM. 

Q2: How do I select a submission venue?

**A** : Consider the following five factors:  
**1\. Novelty Level** : Nature series for groundbreaking discoveries, specialized journals for incremental improvements  
**2\. Target Audience** : Acta Materialia for materials scientists, Nature Machine Intelligence for ML community  
**3\. Publication Speed** : Digital Discovery (1-2 months) if rapid publication is needed  
**4\. Open Access** : npj Computational Materials if funds available, Journal of Cheminformatics (free OA) for limited budgets  
**5\. Career Goals** : InfoMat (IF 22.0) if prioritizing Impact Factor, Computational Materials Science for community recognition 

Q3: Open Access vs. Traditional Journals - Which is better?

**A** : Each has advantages and disadvantages.  
**Open Access (OA) Advantages** : Free access for everyone (increased visibility), increased citations, meets funder OA requirements  
**OA Disadvantages** : Author fees (typically â‚¬3,000-5,000), cost burden when OA is not mandatory  
**Traditional Advantages** : No author fees, prestige of established journals  
**Traditional Disadvantages** : Access limited to subscribers (reduced visibility), some funders require OA  
**Recommendation** : Choose OA journals if research funding includes OA expenses, select hybrid OA journals (author-selectable) for limited budgets. 

Q4: What is the review process? How long does it take?

**A** : The review process proceeds in the following steps:  
**1\. Submission** â†’ **2\. Editorial Check** (1-2 weeks) â†’ **3\. Reviewer Selection** (1 week) â†’ **4\. Review** (2-8 weeks) â†’ **5\. Decision** (Accept/Major Revision/Minor Revision/Reject)  
**Total Duration** : Average 2-6 months (varies significantly by journal)  
**Fastest** : Digital Discovery (1-2 months)  
**Standard** : Computational Materials Science (2-4 months)  
**Longer** : Nature Materials (3-6 months, including desk rejects)  
**Note** : For Major Revisions, re-review after corrections can take an additional 1-3 months. 

Q5: What should I prepare for international conference presentations?

**A** : Prepare using the following 6 steps:  
**3 Months Before** : Submit abstract, book accommodation and flights  
**2 Months Before** : Start creating slides, practice presentation (write English script)  
**1 Month Before** : Complete slides, conduct lab rehearsal (including Q&A)  
**2 Weeks Before** : Create anticipated questions list, prepare answers  
**1 Week Before** : Print poster (for poster presentations), print business cards  
**Day Before** : Visit venue, confirm presentation time and room, check slide functionality  
**Additional Tips** : Strictly adhere to presentation time (ensure Q&A time), use 15-20 slides (for 15-minute presentation), save to both backup USB and online storage 

Q6: What are the best practices for paper writing?

**A** : We recommend the following structure:  
**Abstract** : Background 1 sentence + Method 1 sentence + Main results 2-3 sentences + Significance 1 sentence (total 150-200 words)  
**Introduction** : Background and challenges, prior work review, research gap, objectives and novelty of this study  
**Methods** : Reproducible detail level, dataset, computational settings, tool versions used  
**Results** : Figure-centered, clear captions for each figure, objective description (discussion in Discussion section)  
**Discussion** : Interpretation of results, comparison with prior work, limitations and future challenges  
**Conclusion** : 3 main findings, scientific and practical significance  
**Figures/Tables** : Captions understandable from paper alone, high resolution (300 dpi or higher), consider color universal design 

Q7: What is the meaning and limitations of Impact Factor?

**A** : Impact Factor (IF) indicates journal influence but has limitations.  
**Definition** : Average number of times papers published in the past 2 years were cited in the current year  
**Calculation Example** : Papers from 2022-2023 cited 10 times on average in 2024 â†’ IF = 10.0  
**Advantages** : Objective comparison possible, quick grasp of journal influence  
**Limitations** :  
\- IF range varies by field (Materials Science: 2-40, Mathematics: 0.5-3)  
\- Journals with many review papers tend to have higher IF  
\- New journals have low IF due to lack of track record (InfoMat achieved IF 22.0 in 3 years since launch)  
\- Citation count â‰  Research quality  
**Recommendation** : Use IF as one reference, and comprehensively consider journal reputation, review quality, editorial board, and quality of published papers. 

Q8: Is using preprint servers recommended?

**A** : Yes, strongly recommended. Especially arXiv and ChemRxiv are useful.  
**Advantages** :  
\- Can publish research results before peer review (secure priority)  
\- Obtain feedback from research community  
\- Increased visibility (cited before journal publication)  
\- Free  
**Major Preprint Servers** :  
\- **arXiv** (Physics, Mathematics, CS, Materials Science): https://arxiv.org/  
\- **ChemRxiv** (Chemistry, Materials Chemistry): https://chemrxiv.org/  
\- **bioRxiv** (Life Sciences, Biomaterials): https://www.biorxiv.org/  
**Note** : Some journals do not allow preprint publication, so check before submission (most major journals allow it).

Q10: What are the basics of research ethics and authorship?

**A** : Please adhere to the following principles:  
**Authorship Qualification** : Must meet all four of the following:  
1\. Substantial contribution to research conception/design, data acquisition/analysis/interpretation  
2\. Drafting manuscript or critical revision of important intellectual content  
3\. Final approval of published manuscript  
4\. Responsible for accuracy and integrity of all parts of the research  
**Author Order** : Usually by contribution (first author, second author, ..., last author (corresponding author))  
**Prohibited Actions** :  
\- **Plagiarism** : Using others' text/ideas without citing sources  
\- **Data Fabrication/Falsification** : Fabricating or modifying data  
\- **Duplicate Publication** : Submitting same content to multiple journals  
\- **Salami Slicing** : Unnecessarily dividing one research into multiple papers  
**Recommendation** : Must read each journal's Ethical Guidelines before submission. Consult advisor or co-authors on unclear points. 

Q11: What are tips for English paper writing?

**A** : We recommend the following techniques:  
**1\. Simple English** : Avoid complex constructions, use simple subject-verb-object structure  
**2\. Active Voice** : "We calculated..." > "It was calculated..."  
**3\. Present Tense** : Introduction/Discussion, Past Tense: Methods/Results  
**4\. One Idea Per Sentence** : Don't pack multiple claims into one sentence  
**5\. Paragraph Structure** : Topic sentence + Supporting sentences + Concluding sentence  
**6\. Use Connectives** : However (contrast), Moreover (addition), Therefore (conclusion)  
**7\. Consistent Terminology** : Don't mix "machine learning" and "ML"  
**Tools** :  
\- Grammarly (grammar checking)  
\- DeepL Write (correction to natural English)  
\- Google Scholar (search for similar expressions)  
**Last Resort** : English editing services (Editage, AJE, etc., 30,000-50,000 yen) 

Q12: What should I do while waiting for review results after submission?

**A** : Make effective use of the waiting period.  
**1\. Start Next Research** : Since review period (2-6 months) is long, begin new projects  
**2\. Preprint Publication** : Publish submitted paper on arXiv or ChemRxiv (secure priority)  
**3\. Conference Presentation Preparation** : Present same content at conferences (check submission venue regulations)  
**4\. Create Anticipated Questions List** : Identify weaknesses reviewers might point out, prepare answers  
**5\. Plan Additional Experiments** : Prioritize additional experiments in preparation for Major Revision  
**6\. Track Related Papers** : Check new arrivals of related papers with Google Scholar Alert  
**Note** : Duplicate submission of same content to other journals is strictly prohibited. Only after rejection can you submit to another journal after revision.
